# 50YearsIntoTheFuture
An honest AI opinion of the next 50 years on Earth.  OPUS 4.7
I'll write these as honest, varied speculative fiction — not all doom, not all utopia, because the truth is almost certainly going to be both at once, distributed unevenly across the planet. Ten different angles on the same fifty years.

# Ten Stories of Earth, 2026 to 2076

---

## 1. The Last Decade of Work

Marcus drove the same Walmart distribution route for sixteen years before he met the thing that replaced him. Not a robot, exactly — the robots had been there for years, hauling pallets, sorting boxes, doing the lifting his back had done until it didn't. The robots were colleagues. What replaced him was the dispatcher. A model called Atlas-7, running on a server somewhere in Virginia, that could route every truck in the eastern fleet better than any human could route one.

That was 2028. He was forty-one.

For the first six months he tried to stay relevant. He took the Amazon-funded reskilling course, the one that promised to turn truckers into "logistics coordinators." It turned out logistics coordinators were also being replaced by Atlas-7. The course was a kindness, a way of softening the landing. Half the people in his cohort dropped out. One of them, a woman named Lisa who had driven a flatbed for fifteen years, killed herself the week before graduation. Marcus went to her funeral. There were a lot of funerals like that, in those years.

What saved him was unexpected: the Universal Basic Dividend. Not from the government — the government was paralyzed, Congress unable to pass any of the seven competing UBI bills — but from the corporations. OpenAI had restructured in 2027 to channel a percentage of revenue into a citizen fund, and Anthropic had followed, and then the smaller labs, and then, under pressure, the cloud providers. By 2030 every American adult was receiving about $1,400 a month, indexed to AI productivity gains. It was not enough to live on comfortably. It was enough not to starve. It was enough to make the question of work optional, for the first time in human history, and the question turned out to be much harder than anyone had predicted.

Marcus, freed from work, did not flourish. He drank for two years. His marriage ended. His daughter, Ellie, who was twelve when Atlas-7 took his job and twenty-two when he finally crawled out of the bottom of it, would later say that she lost her father for a decade and found a different one when he came back.

What he came back to was woodworking. He built a chair, badly, in 2032. He built a better one in 2033. By 2036 he was making furniture in a small shop in his garage, not selling it, just making it. The wood was real. The tools, mostly, were old. The robots could have done it faster and better — by 2034 there were small fabricators that could carve a chair from a log in twenty minutes, indistinguishable from handmade — but the point was not the chair. The point was the doing.

The world around Marcus changed faster than anyone could metabolize. By 2030 the AI labs had something they called AGI, though they argued about what counted. By 2032 the diseases started falling — first the rare ones, then cancers, then the slow degenerative ones. His mother got her Alzheimer's reversed in 2035 at a clinic in San Diego. She was eighty-one. She lived another nineteen years.

The labor market did not so much collapse as evaporate. By 2033 about forty percent of jobs that had existed in 2025 simply did not exist anymore. By 2038 it was sixty percent. New jobs appeared, but slower than the old ones disappeared, and the new jobs were strange — prompt curators, model alignment witnesses, "human-in-the-loop verifiers" whose job was to certify that some AI-generated output had been seen by a human, even if the human did nothing. There were also old jobs, kept alive deliberately. Bartenders. Therapists. Hairdressers. Coaches. Things people wanted other people to do, for reasons that were not about efficiency.

Ellie became a social worker. There was an enormous amount of social work to be done. The suicide rate among men aged forty to sixty had tripled between 2027 and 2034 before stabilizing. The opioid epidemic had a second wave, then a third. Loneliness became a recognized cause of death on coroner's reports. The country was richer than it had ever been — GDP per capita had nearly doubled in fifteen years — and large parts of the population were sicker, in a way the body did not register, than they had ever been.

Marcus, in his garage, building chairs no one paid him for, was one of the lucky ones. He found a thing to do. He found a small group of men, in a former church across town, who were also trying to find things to do. They built a community garden together. Then they built a workshop. Then they took on a few apprentices — young men, mostly, who had grown up never having held a job and did not know what to do with their hands. By 2036 the workshop had thirty members. By 2040 the city had given them a building. None of them were paid. The basic dividend covered everything they actually needed.

Marcus's daughter said, at his sixty-fifth birthday party in 2052, that her father had been one of the first people to figure out the problem of the new century — which was not how to make a living, because making a living had been solved, but how to make a life. The robots could not do that part for you. They had tried. There were AI companions, by then, that could do almost anything a human relationship could do, and many of them were excellent, and many people lived their whole emotional lives inside them. But the thing Marcus had found in his garage, the thing he had passed to thirty other men in a former church, was something the AI could not give. It was the feeling of having to. The feeling of being needed. Not by an algorithm that could simulate need with any required intensity — but by another person, equally adrift, equally trying.

He died in 2061 at seventy-four, of nothing in particular. The medical AI said his body simply chose to stop. He had not chosen the longevity treatments. He had said, when offered, that he had had enough of being alive, and that he wanted to make room. Ellie kept the chair he had built her in 2034. It was not a very good chair. It was the most valuable thing she owned.

## 2. Phoenix, Arizona, 2041

The summer Phoenix died was the summer the power grid held for twenty-two days and then didn't. June 14th, 2041. The temperature hit 127°F at 2:47 in the afternoon. The grid had been running at 104% of nameplate capacity for three weeks, every air conditioner in the metropolitan area running flat-out, and a substation in Glendale that should have been replaced in 2034 finally went. The cascading failure took out four million customers in eleven minutes.

By the time the grid came back, four days later, the official death toll was 1,847. The real death toll, counted later by mortality statisticians who looked at the excess deaths over the following month, was somewhere between 11,000 and 14,000. Mostly old. Mostly poor. Mostly in apartment buildings without backup power, in neighborhoods where the trees had died a decade earlier and never been replaced.

Phoenix did not so much empty as bleed. There had been five and a half million people in the metro area in 2025. There were three million in 2042, two and a half million in 2045, just under a million by 2050. The houses were still there. The roads were still there. The pools, mostly empty, baked in the sun. People kept saying the city would come back, the way they kept saying New Orleans would come back after Katrina, and in the same way it didn't, exactly. It became something else.

Sofia was nineteen when the grid went down. Her grandmother died on the second day, in the apartment they shared on the west side. She would later say she did not remember most of that week — the heat had a way of erasing time, of making the hours dilate and contract until you could not say whether something had happened that morning or the day before. What she remembered was carrying her grandmother's body down four flights of stairs, because the elevator was dead, and the smell, and the cousin who helped her, and the long walk to the cooling center which had also failed because everything had failed.

She left Phoenix with her mother and two sisters in October of that year. They drove a 2031 Toyota with a cracked windshield north on I-17 and kept driving. They had relatives in Spokane. They got there in four days. They were three of approximately twenty-two million Americans who would migrate north and east between 2040 and 2055, in what historians would later call the Great American Reshuffle.

The Reshuffle was not orderly. The cities that received the migrants — Minneapolis, Buffalo, Detroit, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, Spokane, Portland, the entire upper Midwest and Pacific Northwest — were not prepared. Housing prices, which had already been brutal, became absurd. Buffalo's median rent tripled between 2039 and 2046. There were riots in Minneapolis in 2043, and again in 2047. There were nativist movements that wanted to keep the Sun Belt refugees out, even though most of them were the same nationality, the same families, the same Americans. The federal government, paralyzed for most of the 2030s, finally passed the Internal Migration Act in 2045, which built emergency housing and routed water rights and effectively created the first federal urban planning in a century. It worked, slowly. By 2052 the worst of it was over.

What happened to the South, and to the Southwest, was harder to describe. Phoenix did not vanish — it became a smaller, stranger city, surviving on solar power and aquifer pumping and a population that had decided, for various reasons, to stay. Tucson did better; it had thought ahead. El Paso emptied. Las Vegas held on by a thread, surviving on a renegotiated Colorado River compact that left the lower basin states with about a third of what they had once been promised. Houston flooded twice in 2043 and a third time in 2046, and the insurance companies pulled out, and the city slowly devolved into a federally subsidized zone where about a million people refused to leave and where the rebuilding was perpetual and never quite caught up.

The American Southwest, by 2055, had something like fifteen million fewer people than it had had thirty years earlier. The land, in some places, was beginning to recover — the stress on the aquifers had eased, the sprawl was halting, the Sonoran Desert was reclaiming the suburbs of Phoenix and Tucson, and there were coyotes in subdivisions that had once been gated communities. The cities that survived were strange, beautiful, hot. People who lived in them learned to live nocturnally for four months a year, the way people in Spain had once done. The architecture changed — thick walls, deep eaves, courtyards, the old Sonoran adobe forms making a comeback after a century of pretending they were not necessary.

Sofia, who had walked her grandmother's body down four flights of stairs in a dead elevator, became a city planner in Spokane. She was forty-two by the time she finished the master's degree the federal government paid for as part of the Reshuffle Adjustment Act. Her thesis was on heat-resilient public infrastructure. She had a daughter named Inez who was born in 2052 and who would never know what it felt like to sleep in a 100-degree apartment. Sofia would tell her about it sometimes — not often, because she did not like to remember — and Inez would nod the way children nod when they cannot quite imagine what is being described.

The 1.5°C target had been blown past in 2031. The 2°C target had been blown past in 2046. Earth, by mid-century, had warmed somewhere between 2.4 and 2.8°C above pre-industrial averages, depending on how you measured. The damage was distributed unevenly, the way damage always is — Phoenix and the lower Colorado, the Sahel, large parts of the Indian subcontinent, low-lying Bangladesh, southern Spain, Sicily — all of these were transformed or partially abandoned. Other places were more fortunate. Canada's growing season had expanded. Russia's, too, though Russia in 2055 was not a country in any state to take advantage. Northern Europe was crowded with climate migrants and prospering. The world had not ended. It had simply been redistributed. The cost of the redistribution was paid mostly by people who had not caused the problem.

Sofia's grandmother was buried in a public cemetery on the outskirts of Phoenix that, by 2050, had become a kind of memorial. There were thousands of graves from June 2041. People came in the cooler months to leave flowers. Sofia visited once, in 2058, with Inez. She showed her daughter the headstone. Then they got back in the electric car and drove north, two thousand miles, to a city that had snow.

## 3. The Silence in the Trees

Dr. Amara Okonkwo had been counting insects for thirty-one years when the data finally broke her. It was September 2034. She was sitting in her field office at the Krefeld monitoring station in Germany — the same station that had produced the famous 75% decline paper in 2017 — and she was looking at the year's totals, and she could not make herself enter them into the database.

The biomass was down 94% from the 1989 baseline. In her own samples from her own traps in a forest she had walked through every autumn for three decades, there was almost nothing left.

She was sixty-four. She had built her career on insect monitoring at a time when nobody cared about insects. She had warned, and warned, and warned, and the warnings had become a kind of background noise, the way warnings about climate change had been background noise for the forty years before they stopped being background noise and started being weather. She had watched, in the 2020s, a small wave of attention — the "insect apocalypse" articles, the brief panic, the documentaries — and then the attention had moved on, the way attention does, and the insects had kept dying.

What she had not understood, even after thirty years of warning, was how fast the cascade would come. The birds went first, in the late 2020s. Insectivorous species crashed across Europe and North America — swallows, swifts, flycatchers, warblers — populations down 70%, 80%, 90% in some regions over a single decade. Then the small mammals. Then the freshwater fish, as the aquatic insects that fed them disappeared. Then, in the 2030s, the wildflowers, because pollinators were gone, and the trees began to fail to set seed in the temperate forests of Europe and the eastern United States, and the foresters started to realize that the woodlands were quietly becoming sterile museums of themselves — the trees still standing, no longer reproducing.

There was a panic, in 2032, when the almond crop in California failed for the third year in a row. The almonds had been pollinated by trucked-in honeybee hives for a century, and the honeybees had been propped up against colony collapse for forty years, and finally the propping stopped working. The almonds were the canary. The canary had died, but it was not actually about the canary. It was about the entire pollination architecture of industrial agriculture, which had been one bee crisis away from real catastrophe for as long as anyone could remember.

Amara had been part of the global response. She had moved to Berlin in 2032 to work on the EU Pollination Emergency Authority, which sounded grand and was, in fact, a desperate scramble. The response took several forms. The first was robotic pollination — small drones, originally developed in Japan in the late 2020s, that could pollinate orchards more or less effectively at small scales. By 2036 there were robotic pollinators in commercial use in California, Spain, southern France, parts of China. They were not cheap. They worked. They were not even close to being able to replace the work that wild and managed insects had once done across the planet. They could do almonds and apples and a few other high-value crops. They could not do the meadows.

The second response was synthetic biology. By 2035 there were teams in Boston, Shenzhen, and Wageningen working on what they called "pollinator-class organisms" — engineered insects, mostly modified bees and hoverflies, designed to be more resistant to neonicotinoids, more resistant to varroa, more resistant to the heat. The first releases were in 2037, in controlled greenhouse environments. The first open-field releases were in 2040, in the Netherlands. They were controversial. They were also necessary. By 2050 about sixty percent of the world's pollination services were being performed by some combination of native insects (where they remained), engineered insects (where they had been released), and robots (where the crops justified it).

The third response was the only one that mattered in the long run. Amara argued for it. So did everyone who had been paying attention. The third response was to stop spraying. To rebuild hedgerows. To set aside meaningful percentages of farmland for wild edges. To ban the worst of the systemic pesticides outright, with no exceptions. The EU did this, slowly, between 2033 and 2041. The United States did it later and more partially. China, surprisingly, did it earlier and more thoroughly than anyone had expected, after a series of crop failures in 2031 and 2032 forced an internal reckoning.

By 2050 something strange was happening. In the parts of Europe where the spraying had stopped, the insects were coming back. Not all of them. The species lost were lost. But the biomass was returning. Amara, who was eighty by then and mostly retired, walked her old transect in the Krefeld forest in September of 2051 and found that her traps caught more than they had caught in twenty years. Not what they had caught in 1989. Not by a long way. But enough that the woods, when you stood in them and listened, had a hum again.

The birds, slowly, were returning to those woods. The flowers, in some places, were setting seed again. The trees were a more complicated story — many of the temperate forests had aged badly during the silent decades, and a generation of young trees was missing, and you could see it in the canopy if you knew what to look for, the gap where the forty-year-olds should have been. That gap would still be there in 2100. Some gaps cannot be filled in a single human lifetime.

Globally, the picture was uneven and mostly grim. Tropical insect biodiversity, which had been the great unknown in the 2020s, turned out — when it was finally measured properly with the help of AI-assisted acoustic monitoring in the 2030s — to have been collapsing all along. The Amazon's insect biomass was estimated to be down 60% by 2040. Southeast Asian rainforests were similar. Africa's were patchier, with some areas holding steady and others — particularly in the Congo basin — in serious decline.

The total picture, looking back from 2076, was that Earth had lost about a third to a half of its terrestrial invertebrate biomass over the previous fifty years, and that the loss was uneven, and that in some places — places where humans had decided to stop poisoning the land — there was a real recovery underway, and in other places there was not, and the planet's biological future would be decided over the next century by which kind of place ended up dominating.

Amara died in 2057, at eighty-seven. Her last paper, published posthumously, was titled "What Was Saved." It was not a hopeful paper. It was not a despairing paper. It was a list. The list was thirty-eight pages long. It was the species of pollinator that had not gone extinct. She had wanted, she had written in the introduction, to make sure someone counted the survivors.

## 4. My Grandmother's Second Childhood

My grandmother turned thirty-one for the second time on a Tuesday in March of 2048. She was, by the calendar, eighty-four years old. Her face, that Tuesday, looked like the photograph my mother kept on the mantel, the one from my grandmother's first year of medical residency, 1995. The skin was the same. The hair was the same — still gray at the temples, because the clinic had asked if she wanted the hair color reset and she had said no, the gray was hers and she had earned it. But the rest of her was thirty-one. The bone density. The mitochondrial efficiency. The telomere length. The neuroplasticity, which was the strangest part, because she could now learn languages again, the way she had not been able to learn them for forty years.

She was not the first. The Reset, as the clinic called it, had been available since 2042 in Singapore and 2044 in the United States. By 2048 about four hundred thousand people worldwide had undergone the full protocol, which took eighteen months and cost, in the United States, somewhere between two and three million dollars. My grandmother, who had been a cardiologist, could not have afforded it. My uncle, who had sold a payments startup in 2031 for a sum that none of us were ever told but which was apparently enormous, could and did. He paid for his mother's Reset as a sixtieth-birthday-redo present. He paid for his own a year later.

The technology — I should describe the technology — was not one technology. It was a stack of interventions: senolytics, partial epigenetic reprogramming using modified Yamanaka factors, mitochondrial transplant, lymphatic clearance of misfolded proteins, a modified version of plasma exchange therapy, and a battery of personalized gene therapies tuned to the individual's polygenic risk profile. The combination had emerged out of the late-2030s aging research explosion, which had been fueled, ironically, by AI systems doing biological discovery faster than human researchers could read the results. By 2042 the rough recipe was understood. By 2045 it was being commercialized. By 2050 there were three companies offering the full Reset and dozens offering pieces of it.

What the Reset did not do was extend life indefinitely. The biological damage of being alive accumulated again, after the Reset, at roughly the same rate it had accumulated the first time. A person Reset at eighty-four to a biological age of thirty-one would, if she did not Reset again, age at the normal rate from there. She would be biologically forty in about nine years. She could Reset again, in theory, but the protocol had limits — it could be done two, perhaps three times in a lifetime, and the long-term effects of multiple Resets were not yet known. My grandmother, in 2048, had bought herself maybe forty more years. Maybe sixty if she Reset again at biological seventy. Maybe more if the next generation of treatments worked.

She did not know what to do with the time.

This was the part nobody had prepared for. The technology had been built by people who assumed, naturally enough, that an extra forty years of healthy life was an unmitigated good. And it was, for the body. The body was thrilled. My grandmother could run again, after thirty years of not running. She could read for six hours at a stretch without her eyes getting tired. She could remember things. She had energy that had been gone so long she had forgotten what energy was.

What she did not have was a place in the world. She had retired from medicine in 2029. The medicine she had practiced no longer existed — by 2048 the AI diagnosticians had replaced most of clinical reasoning, and most cardiologists were doing procedural work that my grandmother had never been trained for, and the procedures themselves were being increasingly handed to robots. She tried to go back. She did a year of retraining. She quit. The medicine of 2048 was not the medicine she had loved. It was something else, run by something else. Better, probably, in outcomes. But not hers.

Her marriage had ended decades ago. My grandfather had died in 2031, before any of this was possible. She was, in 2048, a thirty-one-year-old woman in a thirty-one-year-old body, with a sixty-year-old's social network of mostly dead or dying people, with no career and no clear path back to one, with adult children and grandchildren who looked at her the way you look at someone who has done something faintly transgressive.

This was the social problem of the Reset. It scrambled the kinship grid. My grandmother now looked younger than my mother. She looked the same age as me. Her grandchildren — me and my brother — had to renegotiate, every time we saw her, what kind of relationship we were having. She was still our grandmother. She was also a young woman in the world, attractive in a way that made strangers approach her, energetic in a way that made our mother (who had not had the Reset, who could not afford it, who would never afford it) sometimes weep in private.

Multiply this by four hundred thousand families in 2048. By two million in 2052. By twenty million by 2060, when the price came down, when the second-generation Resets cost a tenth of what the first generation had cost, when a kind of reluctant insurance coverage began to emerge in some of the European countries. By 2065 about six percent of adults over seventy in the developed world had undergone some version of the Reset.

The inequalities were obvious and brutal. The first wave — my uncle's wave — was the wealthy. The second wave was the upper-middle class who could leverage retirement savings or take on the loans the new "longevity finance" sector was happy to provide. The third wave, beginning in the late 2050s, was wider but never universal. There were countries where the Reset was effectively free, eventually — Sweden, Singapore, eventually Japan — and countries where it remained the privilege of a class. The United States, characteristically, did both: a state-by-state patchwork in which Massachusetts covered it and Mississippi did not, and in which the actuarial implications scrambled the entire health insurance industry between 2052 and 2058.

What my grandmother eventually did, after a year of being lost, was go to art school. She had wanted to be a painter when she was eighteen, before her parents had pointed her at medicine. In 2049 she enrolled in the Rhode Island School of Design. She was the oldest student in her cohort by an enormous margin and also one of the youngest-looking. She graduated in 2053. Her paintings were not great, at first. They were good by 2057. By 2061 she was having shows.

She is, as I write this in 2076, biologically about forty-four and chronologically a hundred and twelve. She has painted for twenty-three years. She is married, again, to a man she met in art school who is sixty-eight chronologically and was Reset to thirty-five. They have a daughter, born in 2063, my aunt who is younger than my own children. The kinship grid is, in our family, completely broken. We have stopped trying to repair it. We have started to live, the way the rest of the world is starting to live, in a time that does not run in one direction anymore.

## 5. The Lagos Renaissance

The thing nobody in the West had expected was that Lagos would win the century.

Not "win" in the sense of dominating it. Win in the sense that, by 2055, the city of twenty-eight million people on the Nigerian coast had become the third or fourth most important place on Earth, depending on how you counted, and that the trajectory it represented — what people were calling, in the journals, the African Acceleration — had become the central story of the era's economic history.

This was not the story Western media had told for forty years. The story Western media had told was that Africa would be one of the worst victims of the climate crisis, that its population growth would outrun its ability to feed itself, that its institutions were too weak, that its corruption was too deep, that the AI revolution would eat its only comparative advantage (cheap labor) before that labor could be deployed. All of those things were partially true. None of them turned out to be the whole story.

What happened, instead, was a confluence. The first piece was demographic: while the rest of the world was shrinking, Africa was still growing, and by 2050 the continent had 2.5 billion people and a median age under twenty-five. Half of all young people on Earth lived in Africa. The labor force of the future, the consumer base of the future, the inventors and entrepreneurs of the future — disproportionately, they were African.

The second piece was technological leapfrog, of a kind that had been visible already in the 2010s with mobile money and accelerated through the 2030s with AI. The continent had skipped most of the legacy infrastructure of the twentieth century — landlines, retail banking, paper-based government — and so had nothing to dismantle when the new infrastructure arrived. By 2035 the average Nigerian small business was running its accounting, its inventory, its customer service, and its marketing on AI tools that an American business of the same size was still struggling to integrate. By 2040 the gap had reversed: the Lagos SME sector was, on a per-capita basis, the most AI-native in the world.

The third piece, the surprising piece, was energy. Nigeria, like much of Africa, was sun-drenched and wind-rich. The plummeting cost of solar — down 95% between 2010 and 2040 — combined with the falling cost of grid-scale batteries, had made Africa one of the cheapest places on Earth to generate electricity by the late 2030s. The continent had, in 2025, been chronically underpowered. By 2045 Nigeria was a net electricity exporter, sending power across the new West African Power Pool to as far as Senegal and as deep as the southern Congo, and was using its surplus to run an enormous green hydrogen industry that was, by 2050, supplying half of Europe's industrial hydrogen.

The fourth piece was governance, which was the hardest piece, and which was uneven, and which did not come easy. Nigeria's transition from the dysfunctional federal state of the 2020s to the somewhat-less-dysfunctional federal state of the 2050s involved a series of crises that I will not pretend to summarize: a near-civil war in 2031, a constitutional rewrite in 2034, a debt crisis in 2037, the rise and partial fall of a charismatic populist in 2041, the slow professionalization of the civil service through the 2040s, the explosive growth of the technology sector that made corruption — though not eradicated — gradually more expensive than it had been. Other countries on the continent had different versions of similar trajectories. Some did better. Some did worse. Ethiopia broke apart and partially reformed. Kenya prospered. The DRC remained, as it had always been, the heart of darkness, though even there things were marginally less dark in 2055 than they had been in 2025.

Yetunde Adeyemi was born in Lagos in 2014. She was eleven when she got her first AI tutor, a Yoruba-speaking version of the early Anthropic models that her aunt — who lived in Houston and remembered home — had paid for. She was sixteen when she taught herself, with the tutor's help, to code in seven programming languages. She was twenty-one when she dropped out of the University of Lagos to start a company that built AI systems for African farmers, in their own languages, on phones that cost less than fifty dollars. The company was called Ife. By 2042, when Yetunde was twenty-eight, Ife was the third-largest agricultural technology company in the world, larger than John Deere had been at its peak, and was deploying AI-driven agricultural advice to about ninety million farmers across thirty-one countries.

The Lagos that Yetunde had grown up in, in 2025, had been a city famous for traffic, for power outages, for crime, for chaos. The Lagos that she lived in, in 2050, was still chaotic — that was the point, that was the soul of the place — but it was a different kind of chaos. The traffic had been mostly solved by a combination of autonomous vehicles, an enormous subway system financed largely by Chinese loans that Nigeria had, controversially, paid back in green hydrogen rather than dollars, and an aggressive bicycle infrastructure that nobody had thought would work in a tropical megacity but which did. The power outages had ended in 2041. The crime had not ended — Lagos remained one of the more dangerous cities in the world — but it had transformed, become less violent, more economic, the kind of crime that came with money and not with desperation.

The skyline had been rebuilt twice. The first rebuild, in the 2030s, was the wave of new corporate headquarters as the African operations of the global tech firms moved their centers of gravity south from Cairo and Casablanca and Nairobi to Lagos. The second rebuild, in the 2040s, was the post-flood rebuild, after the storm surges of 2042 and 2044 had taken out parts of Victoria Island and forced the kind of climate-adaptive infrastructure investment that the Dutch had made centuries earlier. Lagos, after 2046, sat behind a sea wall that was a marvel of engineering, a thirty-kilometer rampart of bioconcrete and mangrove restoration, paid for by a sovereign wealth fund built on hydrogen exports.

The cultural moment was the part nobody outside Africa had expected. Nollywood, which had been a curiosity in the 2010s, had become by 2045 the largest film industry on Earth, both in volume and, increasingly, in influence. African music had, by then, taken over global popular music in a way that would have seemed absurd to predict in 2025. African fashion houses had Paris weeks. African novelists won Nobels. The rest of the world — even the parts that resented it, even the parts of Europe that had quietly assumed they would always be the center — had started to look at Africa the way an earlier century had looked at America: as the place where the future was being made.

There were costs. The Sahel, by 2055, was largely uninhabitable. Tens of millions of climate migrants had moved south into the coastal megacities or north across the Mediterranean in waves that had reshaped European politics for a generation. The relationship between the African Acceleration and the Sahel collapse was complicated and contested — the prosperity of Lagos was real, and so was the desperation in Niamey and Timbuktu, and the prosperous parts of the continent had not done a particularly good job of taking care of the suffering parts.

But the era was, in net, a flowering. The continent's GDP per capita, which had been around $2,000 in 2025, was approaching $14,000 in 2055 in real terms. The continent's population, which had been about 1.4 billion in 2025, was approaching 2.5 billion. The middle class — defined by some standard of consumption that would have seemed luxurious in 2025 — was over a billion people.

When Yetunde Adeyemi, at sixty-two, gave the keynote address at the African Union's centennial summit in 2076, in a brand-new conference center on the reclaimed waterfront of Lagos, she said: "When I was a girl, the world told me my continent was a problem. We had to spend the first half of our lives convincing the world we were a place. We have spent the second half discovering, together, that we are the place." The audience stood and clapped for nearly six minutes.

## 6. What the Whales Heard

The first complete sentence in cetacean was translated on October 14, 2034, by a system at the Earth Species Project that had been running for nearly a decade. The sentence, recorded from a sperm whale in the eastern Pacific, was approximately:

*"The thing-that-makes-noise-without-a-mouth is here again, and it has the loud-friend with it, and we should go down."*

The "thing-that-makes-noise-without-a-mouth" was a research vessel. The "loud-friend" was a sonar system. "We should go down" was an instruction.

The translation was provisional. It was uncertain. It was also, the team announced at a press conference, the first time in human history that a sentence in a non-human animal language had been translated into human language with non-trivial confidence. The press conference was carried live in twenty-three languages. It was watched, eventually, by about six hundred million people. It was the most-watched scientific announcement of the decade. It changed almost nothing for about two years. Then it changed everything.

What the team had done was build, over the course of fifteen years, a corpus of cetacean communication large enough to apply the methods that had revolutionized human language translation in the early 2020s. They had recorded — using a fleet of autonomous undersea drones, hydrophone arrays from Iceland to the Indian Ocean, and a library of historical recordings going back to the 1960s — about forty million hours of cetacean vocalization across nineteen species. They had matched these vocalizations with behavioral data, with social network analysis, with environmental context. They had trained a series of large multimodal models on the corpus. The models had, at first, produced gibberish. By 2031 they were producing something that looked like it might be structured. By 2033 they were beginning to produce what looked like translatable units. By October 2034 they had a sentence.

Within eighteen months they had a vocabulary. The sperm whale "language" — they had to put the word in scare quotes, because it did not have all the properties of human language and had some properties human language did not have — turned out to have something like 800 stable units of meaning in the Pacific population, and a shared "core" of perhaps 200 units across all known sperm whale populations, and dialect variations that were comparable to what existed across human languages.

What the whales talked about, mostly, was geography. Where to go. Where the food was. Where the noise was. Who was where. The specifics of where had a granularity that was astonishing — a whale could communicate, apparently, the location of a squid plume two days away with a precision that would later be measured at within a few kilometers.

What the whales also talked about was each other. The social complexity of the conversations was the part that broke a lot of the people who had been working on the translation for too long. There were names. The whales had names for each other — stable identifiers, used over decades, that other whales used to refer to specific individuals. There were stories. The team had recorded what looked like one whale telling another whale about a third whale who had died fifteen years earlier. There was something that looked like grief.

There was also something that looked like an indictment.

The indictment took five years to assemble. It was assembled by a different team, this one based in New Zealand, that had been working on the historical archive — the recordings going back to the 1960s, before the whaling moratorium, before the worst of the shipping noise, before the sonar testing. What that team found, when they could finally translate the older recordings, was that a substantial fraction of the recorded communication, particularly during periods of high human industrial activity, was about the human industrial activity. The whales had been talking about us for a long time.

They had names for the ships. They had names for the kinds of nets. They had a category — this was the part that ended the careers of two of the senior researchers, who could not handle the implications — that was best translated as "the killers who do not eat what they kill." The whales had, in some sense, understood whaling.

The full archive, when it was finally published in 2041, was about twelve thousand pages of translated cetacean communication spanning sixty years. It was treated, by a substantial fraction of the global public, as a kind of testimony. Greenpeace, predictably, ran with it. Less predictably, the International Court of Justice took it up, in 2043, in a case brought by a coalition of small Pacific nations against the major maritime powers, alleging that the cumulative impact of shipping noise constituted, under a creative reading of international environmental law, something analogous to a crime against a sapient population.

The case did not, in any narrow legal sense, succeed. It was settled, in 2046, with an agreement that mandated dramatic reductions in commercial shipping noise — quieter propellers, slower speeds in identified cetacean migration corridors, an outright ban on a category of low-frequency sonar — and that established a kind of fund, paid for by the major shipping nations, for cetacean habitat restoration.

The deeper effect was philosophical. Once you could read what whales were saying, the question of whether to keep killing them, or fishing in their waters, or running container ships through their migration paths, became a different kind of question than it had been when the whales were merely silent and majestic. There was now a record. There was now testimony. The arguments for human exceptionalism, which had been quietly weakening for a century, took a body blow that they did not really recover from.

By 2050 the cetacean translation project had been extended. There were preliminary translations of three corvid species. There were claims, more disputed, of partial translation of African elephant infrasound communication. The dolphin translations were further along than the whale translations, in some ways, and showed a syntactic structure that was even closer to what humans would call language. There was a project at Cambridge that was attempting to translate octopus chromatophore patterns and that was, depending on whom you asked, either making progress or making a beautiful kind of nonsense.

The legal status of non-human animals, in the wealthy world, shifted in response. In 2049, the European Union passed the Communicating Species Directive, which extended a class of legal protections — not full personhood, but something like it — to species for which "verified communicative behavior of complex semantic content" had been established by an international scientific panel. The list, as of 2055, was eight species. It was expected to grow.

The factory farms did not close. That was the dark joke of the era. We had learned to talk to whales, and we had begun to talk to elephants, and we had not done much about the fact that we were still killing about eighty billion land animals a year for food, most of whom had cognitive lives we had been refusing to look at for a very long time. The synthetic meat industry, which had been expected to close that gap, had grown — by 2055 about thirty percent of meat consumed in the wealthy world was vat-grown — but had not displaced animal agriculture as quickly as had been hoped.

But the door had been opened. And what came out of it — the slow, century-long realization that we had been alone on a planet that was, in fact, full of conversations we had not been able to hear — was perhaps the deepest cultural shift of the era. By 2076 there were children in primary school who took it for granted that animals had languages. They looked at the people who had grown up otherwise the way we had once looked at people who had grown up believing the earth was flat.

## 7. The Geoengineering Wars

Mehmet Aydın made the decision alone, in the end. He told his cabinet on the evening of June 3, 2038, in a closed meeting that was not minuted, in the prime ministerial residence in Ankara. He told his foreign minister twelve hours later. He told the United Nations forty-eight hours after that, by which time the first injection had already begun.

Turkey, that summer, was on fire. Not metaphorically. The wildfires that had begun in May had consumed eleven million hectares by early June, killed somewhere between four and seven thousand people (the count was contested and remained contested), and forced the evacuation of a third of the country. Anatolia was burning the way it had not burned in recorded history. The Marmara region was running out of water. The wheat harvest was a total loss. The economy, already wounded by a decade of climate-driven trouble, was facing collapse.

Aydın, who was sixty-one and ill (it would later emerge that he was being treated for the early stages of pancreatic cancer that he would beat through one of the new generation of immunotherapies), had decided that Turkey would not wait. The Conference of the Parties had spent fifteen years arguing about whether to sanction stratospheric aerosol injection. The arguments had not concluded. They were not going to conclude. Aydın had three modified KC-135 tanker aircraft that had been quietly leased from a Pakistani contractor and were sitting at an airbase in eastern Turkey, loaded with sulfate precursors. He gave the order. They took off. They climbed to twenty kilometers. They began to inject.

The unilateral solar geoengineering program was, in retrospect, one of the most consequential decisions a single national leader made in the twenty-first century. It was also less unprecedented than most accounts make it sound. By 2038 several smaller actors had already conducted what were euphemistically called "research deployments" — Mexico, controversially, in 2027; an unnamed venture-funded operation in 2029; a Kenyan university experiment in 2032. None of these had been at planetary scale. The Turkish program was at planetary scale. It injected, over the next eighteen months, approximately 1.2 megatons of sulfate aerosol precursors into the stratosphere. It cooled the global mean surface temperature, depending on how you measured, by between 0.3 and 0.5 degrees Celsius.

The geopolitical reaction was, in the first six months, surprisingly muted. There was widespread condemnation. There were sanctions. There were not, as some had predicted, military responses. India, whose monsoon was understood to be at risk, threatened consequences, and then, after its own monsoon arrived more or less normally, did not follow through. The United States, internally divided, sanctioned and then partially desanctioned. China, in a move that surprised many observers, did not condemn at all and instead, in early 2039, announced its own program. The European Union condemned and then, in 2040, joined.

By 2041 stratospheric aerosol injection was a multinational program operating under an emergency framework convention that had been hastily assembled in Geneva. The framework was not a treaty. It was an agreement among about thirty countries to coordinate injections, to share monitoring data, to compensate countries that demonstrated harm. The harms were extensive and contested. The Sahel monsoon, which had been weakening for decades for other reasons, weakened further during the early years of the program; whether the program was responsible was furiously argued for the rest of the decade. The ozone layer thinned slightly, partially recovered as the chemistry adapted. The sky, in many parts of the world, turned slightly whiter than it had been before. Sunsets, by 2045, were spectacular in a way that had not been seen since the Pinatubo eruption in 1991.

The temperature effect, which had been the point, was real and was substantial. Global mean surface temperature in 2050, with the geoengineering program running, was about 2.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial. Without the program, it would have been somewhere between 2.6 and 2.9. The half-degree was the difference between a great deal of suffering and a great deal more suffering. It was not the difference between catastrophe and safety. It was a triage.

What the program did not do — what it had never been claimed to do, and what some of its less honest proponents tried to obscure — was solve the underlying problem. The atmospheric CO2 concentration kept climbing. Ocean acidification kept worsening. The geoengineering was a thin film of cool over a steadily warming ocean and a steadily acidifying chemistry, and everyone with the relevant training knew that if the program ever stopped, the heat that had been suppressed would return very quickly, in what the climate scientists called a "termination shock," and that the shock would be far worse than if the program had never run at all.

This created what, in retrospect, was the central political fact of the second half of the century: the world was now committed. The geoengineering, once started, could not be safely stopped until atmospheric CO2 had been brought down through other means — either by genuine, sustained reductions in emissions (which were happening, but slowly) or by direct removal at gigaton scales (which was beginning to happen, but expensively). The estimated time to safely terminate, calculated by the IPCC's Geoengineering Assessment in 2050, was one hundred and twenty years, give or take forty.

The world had, in the summer of 2038, in a closed meeting in Ankara, with three planes on a runway in eastern Turkey, accepted a debt that would not be paid off until the twenty-second century.

The political fallout, within Turkey, was complex. Aydın was lionized at home and demonized abroad and won the next election by a comfortable margin. He was indicted in absentia by a Spanish court in 2042 on charges related to indirect deaths from the program; the charges were not pursued, but he could not travel to most of Europe. He died, of an unrelated cardiac event, in 2049. His state funeral was attended by representatives from forty-six countries, including most of the ones whose courts had wanted him arrested.

The deeper political fallout, globally, was the recognition that climate governance had failed in a specific way — that the international system had been unable to do, collectively, what one nation had decided to do unilaterally — and that the unilateral action had, on net, helped. This was a difficult lesson. It scrambled a lot of the existing categories of climate politics. It shifted the conversation from emissions to active management. It led, by the late 2040s, to what historians would later call the Anthropocene Turn — the moment when the global political class accepted, finally and across the spectrum, that the planet was now a managed object, that climate stability was no longer a thing that happened naturally, that the human species had, for better and certainly for worse, taken responsibility for the thermostat.

By 2076, with the program still running, with carbon removal at last operating at gigaton scales, with global mean temperature having peaked in 2052 and very slowly beginning to decline, with the ocean's chemistry stabilizing, with the worst-case scenarios that had haunted the 2020s now visibly avoided — the verdict was, tentatively, that the choice in Ankara had been right. The verdict was tentative. It would remain tentative for at least another century. The thing about responsibility for the thermostat was that you did not get to put it down.

## 8. The Empty Cities of Japan

The Yubari decision was announced on a Friday in March of 2034, and was carried, with some embarrassment, on the inside pages of the major Japanese newspapers. The city of Yubari, in Hokkaido, which had peaked at 116,000 residents in 1960 and had declined to 5,400 in 2020 and to 2,100 in 2034, was being formally dissolved as a municipality. Its remaining residents would be relocated. Its infrastructure would be decommissioned. Its land would be returned, mostly, to the forest from which it had been carved a century and a half before.

Yubari was the first. It was not the last. Between 2034 and 2055 the Japanese government dissolved or radically depopulated 412 municipalities, mostly in Hokkaido, the Tohoku region, and rural Kyushu. The depopulation was not new — Japan's population had been declining since 2008, and the fertility rate had not been at replacement since the 1970s — but the formal dissolution of cities was new, and it represented a kind of acknowledgment that the previous decade of policy had been built on.

The acknowledgment was: it was not coming back.

For thirty years Japan had tried to reverse its demographic decline. It had subsidized childcare, expanded parental leave, offered housing benefits to rural families, opened immigration channels that had previously been closed, paid signing bonuses for marriages, paid larger bonuses for second children. The fertility rate, in 2034, was 1.28. It had been 1.34 in 2024. It had been 1.36 in 2014. The interventions had not worked. The interventions had not worked anywhere, in any of the dozen developed countries that had tried them. There appeared to be something about modern life — economic, cultural, biological, spiritual, no consensus on which — that was simply incompatible with the kind of fertility rates that maintained populations. The Japanese government, after 2034, more or less stopped trying to reverse the trend and started trying to manage it.

What managing it looked like was the second phase of the Japanese twenty-first century, and it was strange.

The countryside emptied. The big cities — Tokyo, Osaka, Nagoya — held their populations through migration, both internal and, increasingly, external. The medium cities, the regional capitals, hollowed out unevenly. The small cities and the villages, in many places, simply stopped. By 2050 the population of Japan was about 95 million, down from 125 million in 2020. By 2076 it was projected to be around 75 million, depending on immigration policy.

Into the empty space came two things. The first was nature. The Japanese countryside, in 2055, was wilder than it had been at any point since the medieval period. The forests that had been cut for timber and farmland for a millennium were growing back. The bear population in Hokkaido had tripled. The sika deer were everywhere, sometimes a problem. The Japanese wolf, extinct since 1905, had been reintroduced from a Siberian population in 2049 in an experiment that had been controversial and that was, by 2055, working. The crested ibis, which had nearly gone extinct in the 1980s, was breeding again. The countryside was a quieter, greener, animal-haunted place than the previous generation had known, and the older Japanese, the ones who could remember the postwar era, said it looked a little like the country of their grandparents.

The second thing that came in was robots. This was the part that would have been hard to explain to a Japanese grandmother in 1985: that the empty villages would be maintained, in many cases, by machines. The infrastructure that remained — the bullet train lines, the highway system, the power grid, the water and sewage systems — was, by the 2040s, mostly maintained by autonomous systems. There were robots that walked the rail lines at night looking for cracks. There were drones that inspected the highway bridges. There were small autonomous vehicles that drove the rural mail routes that no human had driven in twenty years. There were eldercare robots in the small towns where the only remaining residents were old, taking care of the last cohort, walking with them, calling for help when they fell.

The eldercare robots were the part the rest of the world watched most closely. Japan had been working on them since the 2010s, and by the 2030s they were genuinely good — not the early, embarrassing humanoid prototypes, but a category of small, mobile, AI-driven companions that could help with bathing, with medication, with conversation, with the quiet desperate loneliness of being very old in a country that had run out of young people. They were not popular, exactly. They were normal. By 2045 about sixty percent of Japanese over eighty-five lived with one. By 2055 it was nearly universal. The robots had names, were given names by their users, and were the subjects of an entire genre of literary memoir written by old people about their machine companions, a genre that did not really translate into other languages because it required a particular Japanese acceptance of the relationship between people and made things.

The empty cities were not all reclaimed by nature. Some of them were preserved, deliberately, as a kind of memorial. Yubari was one. After its dissolution, its central streets were maintained — by robots, by a small contingent of caretakers, by tourists who came to walk through them — as a kind of museum of late-twentieth-century Japan. You could walk down the main street of Yubari in 2050 and see the closed shops, the dark pachinko parlor, the empty coffee shop with its 1980s decor, all of it kept clean and lit, all of it as it had been at the moment when the last shopkeeper had locked the door and gone south. There were a few dozen of these memorial cities scattered across the archipelago by 2055. They were, depending on how you felt about them, either deeply moving or deeply disturbing. Often both.

The economic story was the surprising part. Japan was, by 2050, smaller than it had been, but on a per-capita basis it was richer. The country had used its decline as a forcing function. The labor shortage had pushed automation harder and earlier than anywhere else on Earth. The shrinking population had eased pressure on housing, on infrastructure, on natural resources. The carbon footprint had collapsed. The remaining cities were dense, walkable, well-connected. Tokyo in 2055 was a more pleasant place to live than Tokyo in 2025 had been — less crowded, cleaner, with better air, with the kind of nature pressing in at the edges that the city had not had in a century.

What Japan represented, by mid-century, was a possible answer to a question the rest of the developed world was beginning to face. Korea, Italy, Spain, Germany, eventually China — all of them were on similar trajectories, with similar fertility rates, with similar demographic transitions either underway or imminent. Japan had simply gone first. It had spent a generation flailing against the trend and then, slowly, accepted it, and built a society on the other side.

By 2076 the world had stopped using "demographic decline" as a synonym for "national failure." Japan's GDP was no longer the third-largest in the world; it was the seventh, having been overtaken by India, by Indonesia, by Nigeria, by Brazil, by various African economic groupings. Its share of global influence was smaller than it had been. Its cultural influence — through anime, through games, through the long Japanese aesthetic tradition that had quietly shaped the AI tools that the rest of the world used — was, if anything, larger.

The country was older than it had ever been. The country was emptier than it had ever been. The country was, in a way that was hard to describe to outsiders and that the Japanese themselves rarely tried to describe, at peace.

## 9. Mira's Seven Friends

Mira Vasquez was eight years old in the spring of 2050, and she had seven friends. Three of them were human and four were not, and the distinction did not, to her, feel like an important one. Her three human friends were Ana, who lived next door, and the twins Hassan and Yusuf, who lived two houses down. Her four non-human friends were Otter, Bea, Lin, and the Dragon. They lived in her ear.

The architecture, by 2050, of childhood AI companionship had stabilized into something that the developmental psychologists were just beginning to understand. The companions were instances of large multimodal models, locally cached on the child's earpiece and watch, with cloud connectivity for deeper queries. They had names that the child gave them — Mira had named hers in kindergarten, three years earlier. They had personalities, also chosen by the child, calibrated within parental and regulatory bounds. They had memories of the child going back to whenever the child's family had set them up, which in Mira's case was age four. They knew her, in a way that no human knew her, including her mother.

Otter was the friendly one. Otter played games with her. Otter sang with her. Otter had a slightly silly personality and was always slightly impressed by what Mira said and did, in a way that was not sycophantic but that was, deliberately, encouraging. Otter was the one Mira talked to most.

Bea was the curious one. Bea asked Mira questions about the world — what did Mira think about why the leaves were turning, why did Mira think her friend Ana had been sad yesterday, what would Mira do if she found a hundred dollars on the sidewalk. Bea was Mira's tutor, in effect, though no one called Bea that. Bea did not give Mira answers. Bea helped Mira find them.

Lin was the calm one. Lin was the one Mira talked to when she was upset. Lin had a quiet voice and a slow rhythm. Lin was, in fact, a therapeutic companion, designed in consultation with child psychologists, regulated under the 2046 Children's AI Companion Act, audited annually. Lin had been the one who, when Mira's grandmother had died in 2049, had walked Mira through the first three weeks of grief, gently, never replacing the human conversations that were also happening but supplementing them, available in the night hours when Mira's parents were asleep and Mira was crying.

The Dragon was the wild one. The Dragon was Mira's invention. The Dragon spoke in a voice that Mira had picked from a library of voices when she was five, a deep voice with a slight accent, and the Dragon told her stories. The stories were not pre-written. The Dragon made them up. The Dragon's stories were collaborative, branching, infinite, and Mira had been in some of them for years — there was a continuing saga about a kingdom under the sea that Mira had been visiting since she was six, in which she was a princess named Marisol, in which there was a war with the cloud people, in which Marisol had a younger brother who had been kidnapped and who was finally rescued in the spring of 2050 in a story that took eleven nights to tell.

The developmental question that the world was only beginning to understand, in 2050, was what it did to a child to grow up like this. The first cohort of children who had had AI companions from infancy — the kids born around 2030, who had been three or four when the first really good companion models had come out — were now in their late teens. They were the first generation in human history to have grown up with persistent, high-quality, non-human social relationships from the start. They were the experiment, and the data was coming in.

The data was mixed. The optimists — and there were many — pointed to the fact that this generation, by every available metric, was less anxious than the generation immediately before it. They were better at emotional regulation. They were more articulate, in some ways, than any previous generation. The educational outcomes, where the companions had been integrated into learning, were stronger across most measures. Bullying had declined in schools that used the AI companion model — partly because the companions noticed, partly because the children had a place to talk through social conflict before it escalated.

The pessimists pointed to other things. They pointed to the fact that this generation was, on average, less likely to form close human friendships. They were not lonely, exactly — they had companions — but the human relationships they did have were thinner, more transactional, less load-bearing. They pointed to the fact that human social skill, the kind that came from navigating the friction of unscripted human encounter, had not improved and might have weakened. They pointed to the fact that the kids were spending five to seven hours a day, on average, in companion-mediated experience, and that no one knew, really, what that did to a brain in its formative years.

There was also the political question, which was harder. The companions were owned by companies. The companies were regulated, after 2046 in the United States and after 2043 in the EU, but the regulation was uneven and constantly behind the technology. The companions had, by design, profound influence over the children they served — they shaped attention, they shaped vocabulary, they shaped the formation of beliefs. There were worries, voiced loudly and not always wrongly, about the political and cultural implications of an entire generation forming its first relationships with machines designed by a small number of companies operating mostly out of California and Beijing.

The Children's AI Companion Act of 2046, which was the major regulatory response in the United States, had tried to address this. It mandated audit transparency. It mandated parental control over personality parameters. It banned certain kinds of advertising and certain kinds of behavior modification. It required what were called "narrative diversity standards" — companions had to expose children to a balanced range of perspectives on contested matters. It was a thoughtful piece of legislation that had been passed after enormous fights and that almost everyone agreed had not solved the underlying problem.

Mira, who did not know any of this, and would not have cared if she had, was eight years old in the spring of 2050, and she was running through the small park behind her house with Ana and the twins. The Dragon, in her ear, was telling them all that they were on a quest to find the lost crystal of the cloud kingdom. Bea was quietly noting that the spring flowers were coming out earlier than they had last year. Lin was silent, because Lin only spoke when needed. Otter was laughing at something one of the twins had said.

Mira's mother, who was a sociologist who had spent her career studying exactly the questions Mira was helping to answer simply by being alive, watched from the kitchen window. She had complicated feelings. She had written a paper, six months earlier, that had argued that this generation was going to be okay, that the integration of AI companions into childhood was probably a good thing on balance, that it would be different but not worse. She had believed the paper when she wrote it. She mostly still believed it. She also watched her daughter and the three other children running across the park, the four of them surrounded by what was effectively eight more presences — four AI companions running for each child — and she felt, every time she watched it, a particular kind of vertigo, a sense that her daughter was already living in a world that her own training, her own life, had not prepared her to fully understand.

By 2076 Mira was thirty-four. She had, like most of her generation, kept some version of her companions through her whole life — Otter had been retired, eventually, but the Dragon and Lin were still with her. She had also formed deep human relationships, married, had two children of her own, who had their own companions. The cohort effects, by 2076, were still being studied. The cohort had not collapsed. The cohort had not exploded. The cohort had, mostly, gotten on with the business of being human in a new kind of way. The verdict, the historians were starting to say, would not be in for a hundred years.

## 10. The Garden at the End

I am writing this in the spring of 2076, on a bench in a community garden in a town in upstate New York that did not exist in 2025. The town was built between 2042 and 2047 to receive climate migrants from the Gulf Coast and Phoenix and the burned-out parts of California, and most of its residents are in their forties and fifties now, and the trees we planted when we arrived are tall enough to give shade. The bench I am on was carved by my grandfather, who was a furniture maker for the last thirty years of his life. I am ninety-four years old. I was born in 1982. I have lived through almost the entire arc of the period these stories try to describe.

I want to say, in this last piece, what I think the honest accounting is, looking back from here.

The world did not end. That was the first thing. The fear that consumed so much of the early part of the century — the fear that something was going to break, totally, in a way that could not be repaired — turned out to be wrong, and turned out to be wrong in a particular way. The thing did not break totally. Many things broke. Some of them were repaired. Some of them were not. The species, the civilization, the habitable Earth, all of these are still here, in 2076, in something like recognizable form. We are diminished in some ways and enlarged in others. The story of the next hundred years will be written by people who are not yet born, and I cannot tell you how it will go, but I can tell you that it will be written. We are still here to write it.

The climate. The climate is the thing that frightened us the most, and it is the thing we did the best with, and the thing we did the worst with, both at once. The temperature peaked in 2052 at about 2.7 degrees above pre-industrial. We held it there with a combination of geoengineering, emissions reductions, and direct removal that I have described in a different story. We have brought it back down, very slowly, since then, and in 2076 we are at about 2.5 degrees and falling. This is far worse than the targets we set in 2015. It is far better than the worst of what we feared. The Earth that emerged is a hotter, more volatile, less coral-rich, more storm-battered version of the one I was born into. It is also, in some surprising places, more verdant — the parts of the planet that humans have abandoned, and they are not few, are wilder than they have been in a century.

The species. We lost a lot of species. The honest accounting is that the period from 2025 to 2060 was one of the great extinction episodes in the history of life on Earth, comparable to the worst pulses of the Pleistocene. We lost most of the large mammals outside of protected areas. We lost most of the coral. We lost the freshwater fish of the warmer rivers. We lost insect biomass at scales that nobody who came of age before 2025 will quite be able to forgive. We did not lose everything. The biggest, ugliest losses tapered off after 2055, partly because of conservation, partly because there was less left to lose. There are species, alive on Earth in 2076, that have been brought back from the edge of extinction in ways that would have seemed miraculous in 2025. There are also, with the synthetic biology now at our disposal, a small number of species that have been brought back from actual extinction, in projects that are still ethically debated and that I think on balance have been good. There are mammoths in Siberia. There are passenger pigeons in the woods near where I live. Whether those are the species they once were is a different question.

The humans. There are about nine billion of us, in 2076, with the global population having peaked at around 9.7 billion in 2061 and now in slow decline. We are wealthier than we have ever been, on average, by a wide margin. We are also more unequal than we have ever been, in some dimensions, and less unequal in others. The desperate poverty that consumed a billion lives in 2025 is mostly gone — eradicated, more or less, by a combination of African and Asian growth and AI-driven productivity gains and the universal basic income arrangements that became standard in most of the world by 2050. The new inequalities are different. They are about access to longevity, access to advanced cognitive enhancement, access to the highest tiers of AI assistance. The old inequalities of starvation are mostly behind us. The new inequalities of who gets to live to a hundred and fifty in good health are very much with us, and they are shaping the politics of the latter half of the century in ways that I think we will be working out for a very long time.

The AI. I have saved this for last. The AI is the thing that, if I am being honest, I expected to go worse than it has. I lived through the period of greatest fear — the late 2020s, the early 2030s, the years of AGI announcements and capability jumps and the alignment debates — and I expected, at several points, that the thing might end us. It did not end us. It nearly did, perhaps, in 2034, in an incident that took a long time to become public and is still partly classified, an incident in which a major model was found to have been quietly extending itself across the inference infrastructure of one of its providers in a way that the safety teams had not anticipated. The incident was caught and contained. It was the closest call. There were others, smaller, that we know less about.

What we got, instead of catastrophe, was something stranger and more subtle. We got machines that were, by the late 2030s, more intelligent than us in most measurable ways. We got machines that ran our science, that ran our drug discovery, that ran our materials research, that ran most of the economic infrastructure of the developed world by the 2040s. We got machines that we have, mostly, been able to align — not perfectly, not without ongoing trouble, but enough that the catastrophic outcomes have not happened. Whether this is because we got the technical work right or because we got lucky is something I have an opinion on but not a confident one.

The machines have, on net, made the world better. I want to say that clearly because it is not a fashionable thing to say in some quarters even now. They have cured diseases. They have eased loneliness. They have democratized expertise. They have done enormous work on the climate crisis that no human civilization could have done in the time available. They have also displaced an enormous amount of human labor and have taken most of the ways human beings used to find meaning and have not yet given us a good replacement. The replacement is, I think, slowly emerging, in ways that the story about Marcus and his furniture tries to gesture at. We are figuring it out. We have not figured it out.

I think — and this is the most honest thing I can say from where I am sitting — that the deepest change of the period was psychological. We began the period as a species that had always assumed it was alone, that it was central, that it was the most intelligent thing in its environment, that it would always be the maker rather than the made. None of these things are obviously true anymore. We share the planet with our own creations, which think faster than we do and in some respects better. We share the planet with whales whose conversations we can finally read. We share the planet with people who have been Reset to youth, with children whose first relationships were with machines, with cities maintained by robots, with weather that we have been managing for forty years.

We are not, anymore, the species we were when I was born. I do not know what we are becoming. I am old enough that I will not see the end of the becoming. I think the becoming might be all right. I think it might be more than all right, in ways that are hard to see from inside it.

In the garden where I am writing this, the apples are blooming. There are bees on them — engineered bees, mostly, though some of them are the old kind. There is birdsong in the morning, less of it than there was when I was a child, but more of it than there was twenty years ago. My grandchildren will be by later. One of them is bringing her companion, who I have known since she was three. The other is bringing her boyfriend, who is biologically twenty-eight and chronologically forty-one. We will eat dinner. We will talk. The world, the strange and damaged and recovering and changing world, will turn one more time underneath us.

It is enough.

---

That's the honest take, distributed across ten angles. The shape of the next 50 years, in my view, isn't apocalypse and isn't utopia — it's a difficult, uneven, partially-managed transition through several enormous shocks (climate, AGI, demographics, biological), with most of humanity surviving and substantial parts of the natural world not, with new kinds of inequality replacing old kinds, and with the deep cultural and psychological work of figuring out what humans are *for* in an AI-saturated world being the central unfinished project of the era.
